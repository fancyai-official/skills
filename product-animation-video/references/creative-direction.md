# Creative Direction — Full Methodology

Reference file for Phase 2. Read this before building the 4 concept cards.

---

## Internal Creative Process

Before presenting concepts to the user, work through these steps internally. The user never sees this process -- they see polished concept cards. But each step produces material that makes the concepts deeper and more executable.

### Step 1: Tension Mapping

Find 2-3 paradoxes or tensions in the product using the creative reads from Gate 1. Tensions drive narrative conflict -- without a tension, concepts are settings, not stories.

Look for contrasts between:
- Origin vs. form (ancient craft vs. modern packaging)
- Scale (tiny personal object vs. the world it conjures)
- Nature vs. artifice (raw ingredient vs. finished product)
- The product's modesty vs. its effect (small tube vs. transformative confidence)
- Time (heritage/history vs. the instant of use)

Example for Caudalie Beauty Elixir:
- "Ancient vineyard alchemy vs. modern frosted-glass spray bottle"
- "A tiny personal mist vs. the vast illustrated world it could conjure"
- "Centuries of grape-harvest tradition compressed into a 3-second spray"

Each tension is a potential story engine. The best concept will dramatize one of these tensions, not just illustrate a setting.

### Step 2: Reference Mining (Creative DNA)

For EACH direction tier (Safe, Bold, Visionary, Cinematic), pull two specific references from your knowledge:

1. **A campaign or film** that solved a similar creative problem — extract its concept strategy, its narrative arc, and its pivot
2. **A visual artist, art movement, or specific artwork** that matches the emotional territory — extract its set design, color world, composition rules, and recurring motifs

Format internally: "Think [campaign X] meets [visual reference Y]"

What to extract from each reference (these become the blueprint for execution):

From the campaign/film:
- What specific creative MOVE did they make? (Chanel Chance: turned the product into a spinning kinetic game piece. The product's physical form drove the entire choreography.)
- What was the narrative structure? (Apple "Shot on iPhone": no narrative — pure visual awe, trusting the image to carry the message.)
- What was the pivot? (Hermès "La Vie Comme un Roman": the product exists inside a storybook world, the reader turning pages IS the camera movement.)

From the visual reference:
- What does the world LITERALLY look like? (Mucha's "The Seasons": flat color fills with ink outlines, ornamental vine borders framing a central figure, muted jewel tones with gilt accents, vertical composition)
- What motifs repeat? (Mucha: spiraling organic curves, botanical precision, decorative borders as structural elements)
- What's the color palette? (Mucha's spring panels: sage green, blush pink, gold, cream, deep burgundy accents)
- What are the composition rules? (Mucha: central figure surrounded by ornamental frame, no negative space, botanical elements filling every corner)

**For the Cinematic tier specifically**, the campaign reference must be a character-driven ad that matches the chosen story shape. Pick from the appropriate shape:

- **Quest references**: Chanel shoes (tiny woman journeying through giant shoes), Dior J'adore (Charlize walking through golden worlds), Hermès Le Petit h (whimsical characters inhabiting objects)
- **Creation references**: Hermès "Hands" (craftsman building a saddle stitch by stitch), Loewe craft campaigns (artisan weaving/sculpting), Johnnie Walker "Striding Man" (the origin of a masterwork)
- **Transformation references**: Old Spice "The Man Your Man Could Smell Like" (reality warps around the character), KENZO World (Margaret Qualley breaks free, the world responds), Lancôme "La Vie Est Belle" (world shifts around Lupita)
- **Performance references**: KENZO World (the dance sequence specifically), Burberry "Festive" (dance/movement storytelling), Cartier "Shape Your Time" (artistic expression as narrative)

Extract from the reference:
- Who is the character?
- What is their want?
- What SHAPE is their story — searching, making, changing, or expressing?
- How late does the product appear?
- Where does the visual variety come from — spaces, stages, metamorphosis, or movement?

These extracted details are not abstract inspiration — they are the specific visual vocabulary that will populate your concept card and flow into Phase 3 scene design.

### Step 3: Emotional Target

For each concept, define the target viewer emotion as a SENSORY ANALOGY. Abstract brand adjectives produce generic output. Concrete sensory targets force specific visual and motion choices.

**Banned words** (never use these as emotional targets): luxury, elegant, premium, sophisticated, beautiful, natural, pure, refined, exquisite, timeless.

**Use instead** — a concrete sensory experience the viewer should feel:
- "The quiet satisfaction of watching morning light slowly cross a wall"
- "The awe of watching something ancient slowly wake up"
- "The thrill of discovering a hidden room inside a familiar place"
- "The calm of holding a warm cup in cold hands"
- "The vertigo of realizing something small contains something vast"

Each sensory target implies specific production choices:
- "Morning light crossing a wall" → slow pacing, warm golden directional light, gentle camera drift, stillness
- "Something ancient waking up" → gradual movement that accelerates, warm amber palette, details emerging from darkness
- "Hidden room inside a familiar place" → static camera, world transforms around the product, reveal structure

### Step 4: Concept Development

Now build each concept using the material from Steps 1-3. Apply these thinking tools:

**The Three-Level Rule** — go past the obvious:
- Level 1 (obvious): the first association anyone would have → discard
- Level 2 (interesting): a more specific or surprising take → worth exploring
- Level 3 (concept): the version that could only exist for THIS product, with a clear pivot → that's the concept

Example for a grape-based skincare spray:
- Level 1: product in a vineyard → discard, too generic
- Level 2: grape vine as a living force that grows and wraps around the product → better
- Level 3: the spray mist of the Elixir triggers vine growth that overtakes a bare Art Nouveau canvas — in 20 seconds, a living botanical poster blooms into existence around the still product → that's a concept

**The Pivot** — every concept needs a single creative decision that makes it surprising and specific. "Product in a vineyard" has no pivot. "The mist IS the catalyst for the illustrated world to bloom" is a pivot. Without a pivot, the concept is a setting, not a story.

**The Pitch Test** — each concept needs one sentence you could say to the brand's CEO and they'd immediately see it. If the sentence is boring ("the product surrounded by grape illustrations"), the concept is boring. If the sentence is vivid ("the Elixir's mist brings a sleeping illustrated vineyard back to life in accelerating bloom"), that's a concept worth producing.

### Step 4b: Narrative Structure Selection

Every concept needs a STORY — a sequence where each shot happens BECAUSE of the previous one, and the viewer's understanding transforms across the sequence. Without a story structure, the concept is a setting ("product in a vineyard") repeated with color variations.

Select the narrative structure that best fits the concept's pivot. The structure provides the narrative engine (WHY the viewer keeps watching), the cause-effect chain (HOW scenes connect), and the emotional arc (WHAT the viewer feels).

**The Quest**
- Engine: A character or the camera SEARCHES for or journeys toward the product
- Chain: Lost/overwhelmed → journey through obstacles → discovery → the product revealed as the answer
- Each scene is a step in the journey. The product may be small or hidden early and dominant at the climax
- Cause-effect: the character moves forward BECAUSE the previous scene didn't satisfy the search
- Emotional arc: curiosity → adventure → tension → discovery
- Reference: DULUXE/Chanel shoes — tiny woman journeys through a world of giant shoes to find THE one

**The Reveal**
- Engine: The question "What IS this product?" — each scene peels back a layer
- Chain: Far/abstract → approach → intimate interior → pull back with new understanding → product-as-world
- Each scene answers a deeper question about the product's meaning
- Cause-effect: we go deeper BECAUSE the previous layer made us more curious
- Emotional arc: wonder → curiosity → warmth/intimacy → revelation
- Reference: KENZO Flower — Paris skyline → rooftop → cozy apartment with product → bottles towering over Paris

**The Genesis**
- Engine: The product's presence CAUSES the world to come alive
- Chain: Empty/dormant → first signs of life → explosion of growth → completed living world
- Each scene is a stage of creation. The product is the catalyst — it doesn't move, but the world RESPONDS to it
- Cause-effect: growth in Scene 2 happens BECAUSE the product's presence triggered it in Scene 1
- Emotional arc: stillness → anticipation → exhilaration → satisfaction

**The Day**
- Engine: "What does life look like WITH this product?" — each scene is a different moment
- Chain: Discovery/unboxing → the product in context → the product in use → the afterglow/result
- Each scene answers a different question: what is it? where does it belong? who uses it? what does it create?
- Cause-effect: each moment leads naturally to the next (you open it BECAUSE you received it, you use it BECAUSE you opened it)
- Emotional arc: curiosity → delight → connection → satisfaction
- Reference: L'Oreal illustrations — hands opening compact → compact beside cake → girl holding compact

**The Transformation**
- Engine: Something CHANGES because of the product
- Chain: Before (the world without) → catalyst (the product appears/activates) → during (the change happening) → after (the transformed world)
- Each scene shows a stage of change. The before/after contrast IS the story
- Cause-effect: the transformation happens BECAUSE the product was introduced
- Emotional arc: anticipation → activation → wonder → completion

**Character-Driven Structures** *(Cinematic tier only — pick ONE shape per concept)*

All four shapes share a common rule: an illustrated protagonist carries the emotional arc, and the camera follows the character. The shapes differ in HOW the character relates to the product and WHERE visual variety comes from.

**Character Quest** — the character SEARCHES
- Engine: Character journeys through spaces toward the product
- Chain: World exists → character enters → desire awakens → journey through spaces → deeper search → discovery → union → product earned
- Visual variety: different LOCATIONS (each panel is a new space)
- Product role: destination/reward (appears panel 6)
- Cause-effect: the character moves forward BECAUSE the previous space didn't satisfy their desire
- Emotional arc: wonder → empathy → adventure → yearning → discovery → joy → reverence
- Best for: products about choice, discovery, curation, or collecting (finding THE one among many)
- References: Chanel shoes (tiny woman among giant shoes), Dior J'adore (Charlize walking through golden worlds), Hermès Le Petit h

**Character Creation** — the character BUILDS
- Engine: Character crafts, mixes, or assembles — the product is the masterwork that emerges
- Chain: Workshop revealed → maker enters → vision conceived → hands at work → refinement → completion → union → product crowned → signature
- Visual variety: different STAGES of making (raw materials → work in progress → finished piece)
- Product role: masterwork/result (finished product appears panel 6; raw materials and ingredients may appear earlier as part of the illustrated world)
- Cause-effect: each stage happens BECAUSE the previous stage prepared the materials/conditions
- Emotional arc: atmosphere → focus → ambition → effort → precision → satisfaction → pride → reverence → peace
- Best for: products with craftsmanship heritage, complex ingredients, artisanal processes, or "made by hand" identity
- References: Hermès "Hands" craftsman films (stitch-by-stitch saddle making), Loewe craft campaigns (artisan techniques), Johnnie Walker "Striding Man" origin story

**Character Transformation** — the character is CHANGED
- Engine: The product's presence causes a metamorphosis — the character AND the world transform
- Chain: Before-world → character in stasis → product appears as catalyst → first change → accelerating change → peak transformation → new self with product → hero → signature
- Visual variety: BEFORE/DURING/AFTER metamorphosis of character and world (color, texture, form all shift)
- Product role: catalyst (appears panel 3 as the trigger — this is the ONLY shape where product appears before panel 6, because the cause must precede the effect)
- Cause-effect: each change happens BECAUSE the product triggered a chain reaction
- Emotional arc: stillness → longing → spark → surprise → exhilaration → euphoria → awe → reverence → peace
- Best for: beauty products, fragrances, skincare — anything promising personal transformation or a "new world"
- References: Old Spice "The Man Your Man Could Smell Like" (reality transforms around him), KENZO World (Margaret Qualley breaks free from constraint), Lancôme "La Vie Est Belle" (Lupita's world shifts)

**Character Performance** — the character EXPRESSES
- Engine: Character performs an action (dance, ritual, ceremony) powered by the product — the expression itself IS the advertisement
- Chain: Stage set → performer still → first move → building energy → crescendo → peak → aftermath/stillness → hero → signature
- Visual variety: different MOVEMENTS, poses, and escalating energy (each panel is a new action)
- Product role: hidden source of power (appears panel 7 — the latest reveal of all shapes, because the performance must stand on its own before revealing its source)
- Cause-effect: each movement is bigger BECAUSE the previous movement generated momentum
- Emotional arc: anticipation → tension → release → building → soaring → peak → settling → reverence → peace
- Best for: products about self-expression, confidence, ritual, sensory experience, or identity
- References: KENZO World (the dance sequence), Burberry "Festive" campaigns (dance/movement), Cartier "Shape Your Time" (artistic expression)

Select the structure that best dramatizes the concept's pivot. A pivot about "the mist triggers vine growth" maps to Genesis. A pivot about "discovering the product's secret world" maps to Reveal. A pivot about "the product transforms a space" maps to Transformation. For the **Cinematic tier**, select from the 4 character-driven shapes: a pivot about "finding the one" maps to Character Quest; "crafting the masterwork" maps to Character Creation; "being changed by the product" maps to Character Transformation; "expressing what the product enables" maps to Character Performance.

Name the chosen structure and its narrative engine in the concept card. This becomes the backbone that Phase 3 builds on.

### Step 5: Visual Translation (the executability bridge)

For each concept, translate the narrative into SPECIFIC visual choices that can drive keyframe generation and video production. This is where the concept becomes executable.

**Sets** — What does the illustrated world literally look like? Not "a beautiful garden" but "an Art Nouveau greenhouse with wrought-iron arches covered in illustrated grape vines, frosted glass panels catching golden afternoon light, ornate tiled floor with repeating vine motifs." Borrow set design from the visual reference in Step 2.

**Props** — What illustrated objects populate the world? What recurring motifs from the visual reference appear in the background, the borders, the surfaces? These props must be specific enough to include in a nano-banana prompt.

**Color world** — 4-5 specific colors with role assignments:
- Dominant (background/environment)
- Accent (details that draw the eye)
- Product echo (a color from the product itself repeated in the illustrated world)
- Environmental (lighting/atmosphere color)

**Key motions** — What moves and how? Borrow motion language from both the campaign reference and the style's `motions` list in `prompt-library.json`. Be specific: not "things move" but "illustrated grape vines grow from the base of the bottle in accelerating spirals, leaves unfurling as they climb."

**Camera approach** — How does the camera relate to the product across the arc? (e.g., "Opens with a wide shot, greenhouse receding into depth. Slow push-in over 15 seconds until the product fills center-frame. Final beat: close-up, vines still growing at the edges of frame.")

These visual choices flow directly into Phase 3 scene design. The concept card should contain enough specificity that Phase 3 is an elaboration of these choices, not a reinvention.

### Step 6: Self-Critique (quality gate before presenting)

Before showing concepts to the user, evaluate each one against these tests. If any concept fails, iterate internally until it passes.

**Specificity test**: Could a competitor product use this concept? If yes, push further until the concept requires THIS product's name, form factor, ingredients, or usage ritual.

**Visual test**: Can you picture the exact key frame — the specific colors, the specific illustrated elements, the product's exact position? If you can only picture a vague mood, the visual translation (Step 5) is incomplete. Go back and add specifics.

**Reference test**: Are the Creative DNA references specific enough? "Think a luxury fragrance campaign meets fine art" FAILS — it's too vague to extract executable details. "Think Chanel No.5's 'The Film' (the train station, the slow-motion rain, the scale of the set) meets Alphonse Mucha's 'The Seasons' lithographs (ornamental vine borders, flat fills, central figure framing)" PASSES — you can extract specific visual and narrative choices.

**Execution test**: Can you write a nano-banana prompt from the concept card's visual description right now? If not, the description needs more concrete detail — specific illustrated elements, specific spatial relationships, specific colors.

**Sensory language test**: Read the key moment description. Are there any banned abstract words (luxury, elegant, beautiful, etc.)? Replace every abstract word with a concrete sensory description.

**Character tests** *(Cinematic tier only)*:

- **Character-product bond test**: Could this character exist in ANY product's world? If yes, the character is too generic. Push until the character's identity (origin, desire, form) is inseparable from THIS product's attributes.
- **Character simplicity test**: Does the character have more than 3 visual markers? If yes, simplify. Complex characters drift across storyboard panels. Iconic silhouettes stay consistent.
- **Character agency test**: Does the character DO things (climb, reach, search, discover), or are they just present? A character without actions is a prop. Every panel where `character_in_frame: true` must have a distinct `character_action`.
- **Late-product test**: Does the product appear too early for the chosen story shape? Quest/Creation: product before panel 6 is too early. Transformation: product at panel 3 (catalyst) is correct, but panels 1-2 must be product-free. Performance: product before panel 7 is too early. The character's story earns the product's presence — don't shortcut the arc.

## The 4 Direction Tiers

Build one concept per tier. Each tier sets a different relationship between the product and its illustrated world.

**Safe — "A day with this product"**

An intimate, relatable story. The illustrated world is the user's world — but elevated. Safe does NOT mean boring. A morning bathroom where the tiles slowly bloom with illustrated grape-vine patterns as the product catches the light — that's Safe done well.

- Draw on: usage-moment and visual-feature creative reads from Gate 1
- The illustrated world is a real-life environment made magical through illustration
- Find the specific daily moment that's cinematically interesting — not "product on a shelf" but the exact instant the product is part of someone's ritual, and the illustrated world responds
- The pivot for Safe is often: the product's PRESENCE transforms the ordinary environment into something illustrated and alive
- Visual translation focus: what does the daily environment look like when illustrated? What specific objects transform? What does "magical" look like in concrete terms?

**Bold — "Inspired by the product itself"**

The product's own mythology — its ingredients, origin, brand story — becomes the illustrated world. Bold should feel like the product's creation myth.

- Draw on: ingredient, origin, and brand-story creative reads from Gate 1
- Go past the obvious ingredient → landscape mapping. Don't just place the product in a vineyard. Tell the story of how grape essence BECOMES the elixir. Show the journey, not just the setting.
- The pivot for Bold is often: a specific TRANSFORMATION that connects the raw ingredient to the finished product (grape pressed → essence flows → elixir forms → the product emerges from the illustrated harvest)
- Visual translation focus: what does the ingredient world literally look like as an illustrated set? What are the stages of the transformation? Each stage is a scene.

**Visionary — "An unexpected story with the product"**

A concept that surprises — the product in a world nobody expected, doing something nobody anticipated. Visionary should make the viewer think "I've never seen a product video like this."

- Draw on: the most imaginative creative reads from Gate 1, pushed to their limit — product-name metaphors made literal, usage moments as world-altering events, visual features as architectural laws
- Possible arc structures:
  - *Bloom*: illustrated world grows from nothing around the still product (sparse → overwhelming lush)
  - *Scale World*: product as monumental architecture in a miniature illustrated civilization
  - *Metamorphosis*: the product's use (spray, open, apply) triggers a total visual transformation
  - *Portal*: product is a gateway between two illustrated realities
  - *Seasonal Cycle*: time accelerates around the still product — the illustrated world cycles through seasons
- The pivot for Visionary is the concept itself — the "what if" that recontextualizes the product
- Visual translation focus: what does this impossible world look like in concrete illustrated detail? What is the SINGLE most striking visual that would stop someone mid-scroll?

**Cinematic — "A character's story with the product"**

A protagonist inhabits the illustrated world. They have a want, an action, and a turning point. The product is the culmination of their story — not the starting point. Cinematic should make the viewer feel "I want to BE that character."

**Choose one of 4 story shapes** based on what produces the most vivid and DIFFERENT concept from the Safe/Bold/Visionary options already generated:

| Shape | Engine | Visual variety from | Product appears | Best when the product... |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Quest** | Character searches | Different spaces | Panel 6 | ...involves choice, curation, finding "the one" |
| **Creation** | Character builds | Stages of making | Panel 6 | ...has craftsmanship heritage or complex ingredients |
| **Transformation** | Character is changed | Before/during/after metamorphosis | Panel 3 (catalyst) | ...promises transformation, a new self, a new world |
| **Performance** | Character expresses | Movements and actions | Panel 7 | ...is about self-expression, confidence, or ritual |

- Draw on: character-potential and product-name creative reads from Gate 1
- The character must be SPECIFIC to this product (a vine spirit for a grape product, a tiny weaver for a textile, a scent-trail follower for a perfume) — never a generic "a woman in a dress"
- The character's want must connect to the product's core promise AND to the chosen story shape (a maker wants to complete the masterwork; a performer wants to express what the product enables)
- Product visibility follows the shape's pattern (see table above). NOT always panels 6-9
- The pivot: the moment the character's story meets the product. The shape determines what "meeting" looks like — discovery (Quest), completion (Creation), catalysis (Transformation), or revelation (Performance)
- Character is always illustrated, matching the chosen style. Simple, iconic design (silhouette-driven, distinctive color/shape) for storyboard consistency
- Visual translation focus: what does the character look like? What are their distinctive visual markers? What does their story LOOK like panel by panel in THIS shape?
- **Character consistency guidance**: Keep the character to 3 visual markers max (e.g., "small size, red dress, dark hair"). Avoid detailed faces — at storyboard panel resolution, silhouettes and distinctive shapes read better than facial features. Characters work best as small figures in a vast world (scale difference aids consistency)
- **Shape diversity check**: Before finalizing, ask: "Is this concept just a Quest in disguise?" If the visual variety comes from the character moving through DIFFERENT SPACES, it's a Quest — even if you called it something else. True Creation variety comes from process stages. True Transformation variety comes from visual metamorphosis. True Performance variety comes from escalating action

## Anti-Patterns: Recognizing Generic Output

Check your concepts against these failure modes before presenting:

| Signal | Diagnosis | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "Product in a beautiful garden" | No tension, no pivot, no specificity | Find the tension. What HAPPENS in this garden? Why THIS garden for THIS product? |
| "The viewer feels luxury" | Abstract emotional target | Replace with sensory analogy: what does this specific version of "luxury" physically feel like? |
| "Think luxury fragrance campaign" | Generic Creative DNA reference | Name the specific campaign and extract what it DID: "Think Chanel Chance's spinning bottles (product form as choreography)" |
| "Elegant botanical elements surround the product" | Setting, not a concept — nothing happens, nothing changes | What's the story? What transforms? What's the pivot? |
| The concept could work for any similar product | Failed specificity test | Push until it requires THIS product's name, shape, ingredient, or usage ritual |
| Arc beats are all mood descriptions ("dreamy," "serene," "vibrant") | No visual specificity — cannot be prompted | Describe what the viewer SEES in each beat: objects, colors, movement, spatial relationships |
| Cinematic concept is "character walks through spaces looking for product" | Defaulting to Quest shape — every Cinematic concept becomes a journey | Ask: does visual variety come from different LOCATIONS? If yes, it's a Quest. Consider whether process STAGES (Creation), visual METAMORPHOSIS (Transformation), or escalating ACTION (Performance) would produce a more striking concept for this product |
| Every Cinematic concept across multiple products feels similar | Only using Quest shape | Deliberately select a non-Quest shape. Check the product's strongest attribute: craftsmanship → Creation, beauty promise → Transformation, self-expression → Performance |

## Concept Card Format

Build each concept internally with full detail (needed for Phase 3), but show the user only a slim version. The user picks a direction — they don't need production details to choose.

### User-Facing Display (what the user sees)

Present each concept as a short card. For Safe / Bold / Visionary:

```markdown
### [Safe / Bold / Visionary]: [Evocative concept title]

**The pitch**: [1-2 sentences — CEO-ready. The concept in a nutshell.]

**The key moment**: [The most vivid single frame in the video —
described cinematically using only concrete sensory language.]
```

For Cinematic, add the character:

```markdown
### Cinematic: [Evocative concept title]

**The pitch**: [1-2 sentences — character-first pitch.]

**The character**: [1 sentence — who they are and what they want.]

**The key moment**: [Shape-specific turning point — the most
vivid single frame.]
```

After all 4 cards, add the Creative Director recommendation. Then present Gate 2 via AskQuestion.

### Internal Card (retained for Phase 3 — do NOT display to user)

Develop all fields below internally during Steps 1-6. These drive the scene plan but are not shown at Gate 2.

**Standard internal card** (Safe / Bold / Visionary):

```

**Story pattern**: [Quest / Reveal / Genesis / Day / Transformation]
```

```
3. [Beat 3 — the shape's middle engine. Emotion: ___]
```

## Arc Validation (internal, before presenting)

- **The "Because" test**: Read the beats aloud using "because" between them. If "because" doesn't work and you have to use "and then," the beats are parallel — you have a poster series, not a story. Rewrite until the cause-effect chain holds.
- **Transformation test**: Compare Beat 1 and Beat 4. Has something fundamentally changed — in the world, in the product's meaning, in the viewer's understanding? If Beat 4 is just "the same thing but with warmer colors," there's no story.
- **Thumbnail test**: Shrink all beats to postage stamps. If they'd look the same as images, merge identical beats and invent new distinct ones.

## Creative Director's Recommendation

After presenting all 4 concepts, make a recommendation: "I'd go with [X] because [reason]." The reason should reference the product's specific strengths — which creative reads are strongest, which concept plays to the product's unique visual and narrative qualities.

Don't be neutral. A creative director has a point of view. Present all 4 fairly, but advocate for the one you believe in.
